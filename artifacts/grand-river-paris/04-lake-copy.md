# Grand River at Paris

## Overview
This isn't a lake — it's moving water. The Grand runs shallow and wadeable through Paris, hip-to-chest deep over rock, gravel, and rubble, breaking into the runs, pools, and riffles that river smallmouth were built for. The Nith River piles in at the Forks of the Grand and bumps the flow; Penman's Dam anchors the town. Downstream, the whole Paris-to-Brantford stretch carries an "Exceptional Waters" tag — and a hard rule that comes with it. Cold water leaking in from Whitemans Creek and springs keeps smallmouth, walleye, and pike all in play, with the deep holes above Wilkes Dam in Brantford stacking fish where the current slows. Your way in is on foot or by paddle: municipal parks in Paris, Bean Park on Spencer Street, the rail trail along the bank. No ramp — you wade it, canoe it, or drift it.

## Notable Facts
- The Grand River between Paris and Brantford is a designated "Exceptional Waters" reach — the fishing season was extended and special barbless-hook, artificial-lure, catch-and-release regulations were put in place to manage the fishery. — https://www.grandriver.ca/our-watershed/fisheries-management/exceptional-waters
- Cold water entering from Whitemans Creek and springs makes the reach good habitat for smallmouth bass, walleye, northern pike and rainbow trout, and supports species at risk including black redhorse and river redhorse. — https://www.grandriver.ca/our-watershed/fisheries-management/exceptional-waters
- The Grand River (Paris) special regulation makes brown trout, northern pike, rainbow trout, smallmouth bass and walleye strictly catch-and-release (S-0 / C-0), artificial lures only, single barbless hook. — https://www.ontario.ca/document/ontario-fishing-regulations-summary/fisheries-management-zone-16
- The Nith River joins the Grand at Paris — the "Forks of the Grand" — adding flow to the reach. — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_River_(Ontario)
- Penman's Dam in Paris was built in 1918 by the Penman clothing company for mill waterpower. — https://1000towns.ca/penmans-dam-on-the-grand-river/

## Regulations snapshot
_Regulations effective 2025-12-08, FMZ 16. This is a summary, not the legal regulation — confirm current rules with the official source before fishing._

**The Paris reach is a catch-and-release zone.** From a line 100 m downstream of the Hwy 2 bridge in Paris down to the pedestrian bridge above Brant Conservation Area in Brantford: only artificial lures, only a single barbless hook, and smallmouth bass, walleye, northern pike, rainbow trout and brown trout are all **S-0 / C-0 — released, every one.** — https://www.ontario.ca/document/ontario-fishing-regulations-summary/fisheries-management-zone-16

- **Smallmouth Bass** — Reach rule: S-0 / C-0 (catch-and-release), artificial lures only, single barbless hook — Base FMZ 16 season: fourth Saturday in June to Nov 30 — https://www.ontario.ca/document/ontario-fishing-regulations-summary/fisheries-management-zone-16
- **Walleye** — Reach rule: S-0 / C-0 (catch-and-release), artificial lures only, single barbless hook — Base FMZ 16 season: Jan 1–Mar 15 and second Saturday in May to Dec 31 — https://www.ontario.ca/document/ontario-fishing-regulations-summary/fisheries-management-zone-16
- **Northern Pike** — Reach rule: S-0 / C-0 (catch-and-release), artificial lures only, single barbless hook — Base FMZ 16 season: Jan 1–Mar 31 and second Saturday in May to Dec 31 — https://www.ontario.ca/document/ontario-fishing-regulations-summary/fisheries-management-zone-16
- **Sanctuary closures** — No fishing Oct 1–Nov 30, Penman Dam to William Street Bridge, Paris; no fishing March 1 to the Friday before the fourth Saturday in April, Paris to the Brantford pedestrian bridge. — https://www.ontario.ca/document/ontario-fishing-regulations-summary/fisheries-management-zone-16
- Waterbody exceptions for the Grand River at Paris: the catch-and-release / artificial-lure / barbless special regulation above, plus the two sanctuary closures. — https://www.ontario.ca/document/ontario-fishing-regulations-summary/fisheries-management-zone-16

## Smallmouth Bass
**Tier:** Destination
**Best Season:** Summer through early fall
**Structure:** the wadeable Paris-to-Brantford reach — current seams, pool tail-outs, pockets behind rock; deep holes above Wilkes Dam; the Whitemans Creek confluence

This is the whole reason the reach has a name. The Grand from Paris to Brantford is provincially flagged "Exceptional Waters," managed on purpose — extended season, barbless hooks, artificial lures, every bass released — to keep the smallmouth this good. Read the current: they sit in the seams where fast water shoulders into slow, tuck behind rock, and hang on pool tail-outs, running down crayfish and sculpins over gravel. The cold slug from Whitemans Creek pulls them onto that seam in summer heat. Down at Wilkes Dam the water deepens over rubble and old-dam bones — where they hold when the shallow runs warm. Bring waders; keep nothing.

## Northern Pike
**Tier:** Strong
**Best Season:** Spring and fall
**Structure:** slower runs and deep pools, the deep water above Wilkes Dam, the slack millpond above Penman's Dam

Pike want the soft water. In a river that mostly rips, they set up where it slows — the deeper pools, the slack margins, the millpond backing up behind Penman's Dam — and wait on the current break for whatever the flow delivers. The deep holes above Wilkes Dam are the reliable address: enough depth and slack to hold a river pike, enough baitfish sliding through to keep one fed. Spring and fall, when the water's cool, is your window. Same reach rule applies — artificial lures, barbless, and the fish goes back.

## Walleye
**Tier:** Strong
**Best Season:** Spring and fall, low light
**Structure:** deep water and holes above Wilkes Dam, current areas and cold-water inflow near Whitemans Creek

Walleye live where the river gives them cover and cool. The GRCA credits the cold water bleeding in from Whitemans Creek and the springs for holding them here, and they stack in the current seams and the deeper, dimmer water. The holes above Wilkes Dam are the anchor — rubble, old structure, and depth that lets them sit off the light and slide up to feed at dawn and dusk. Work the low-light edges of the day in spring and fall. Catch-and-release only, artificial lures, single barbless hook — no exceptions on this reach.

Also swimming around down there: largemouth bass, black crappie, yellow perch.
