# Wildwood Reservoir

## Overview

Wildwood is flood control first, fishery second — and the flood control runs the fishing. It's nine kilometres of drowned Trout Creek valley, S-shaped and mostly shallow, impounded behind the Wildwood Dam between Stratford and St. Marys by the Upper Thames River Conservation Authority. One rhythm governs everything: the reservoir fills to summer pool by late spring, then draws down all season — a 3.5-metre swing that strands the upper arms and, by fall, the launch itself. The deep water sits at the dam end, and that's where fish stack as the reservoir shrinks. Pike prowl the basin and the flooded spring shallows; yellow perch school the same deep dam water through the ice. Smallmouth and largemouth swim here too, harder to pin down. Your way in is the Wildwood Conservation Area launch off Line 9 near St. Marys — motorboats allowed, no personal watercraft, boating May through October.

## Notable Facts
- Wildwood Dam (built 1962–1965) was among the UTRCA's first major flood-control works; during the February 2018 flood its operations cut Trout Creek's peak flow from 65 m³/s to 21 m³/s (a 68% reduction), helping keep the North Thames below the St. Marys floodwall. — https://thamesriver.on.ca/wp-content/uploads/WildwoodDam_QA-June2019.pdf
- In dry summers, releases from Wildwood Reservoir supply 25–50% (or more) of the flow in the North Thames River. — https://thamesriver.on.ca/water-management/flood-control-structures/wildwood-dam/
- Wildwood is one of only three large boatable UTRCA reservoirs (with Fanshawe and Pittock), all promoted as perch, bass, and pike fisheries. — https://thamesriver.on.ca/parks-recreation-natural-areas/fishing/
- No stocking record exists for Wildwood Lake/Reservoir or Trout Creek in the official MNR Fish ON-Line FMZ 16 dataset; the fishery is self-sustaining. — https://www.lioapplications.lrc.gov.on.ca/services/FishONLine/StockingList.aspx?json=true&fmz=16&stock=AllSpecies&diagnostics=false&lang=en

## Northern Pike
**Tier:** Strong
**Best Season:** Open water from the second-Saturday-in-May opener; also a winter ice fishery
**Structure:** dam basin (deepest water, west end); spring-refill drawdown shallows up the arms

Pike own the deep end. When the spring refill floods the sandy banks up the S-shaped arms, they slide into the fresh-drowned vegetation to spawn, then spend the open-water season working the weed and flat edges. Come winter the whole game shifts to the dam basin — the deepest water left after drawdown, where ice anglers pull them from the lowest holes, around 15 feet down. They're the most-logged fish on the reservoir, so start where the water stays deep: the west end, hard against the dam.

## Yellow Perch
**Tier:** Strong
**Best Season:** Open all year; the standout is the winter ice fishery in the dam basin
**Structure:** dam-wall basin, 12–15 ft

Winter is perch time. As the reservoir drops to its holding level, the perch stack into the dam-wall basin and hold over 12 to 15 feet — a school you can practically walk to, since anglers park roadside by the dam and drill a line of holes straight out from the wall. Minnows worked just off bottom do it. They're also the pike's grocery store, which is why both species crowd the same deep basin once the shallows drain away.

Also down there: smallmouth and largemouth bass — well represented in the catch logs, but no source pins either to a named spot on this reservoir.
