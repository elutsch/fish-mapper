# Species Sub-Guides: Wildwood Reservoir

## Northern Pike
**Parent slug:** northern-pike
**Tier:** Strong

### Lede
Follow the water up the arms in spring. As the reservoir climbs toward its 6.55-metre summer pool, it re-floods the sandy, drawn-down banks of the S-shaped upper valley, and pike push into that fresh vegetation to spawn — the reservoir's calendar and the pike's run on the same clock. By the second-Saturday-of-May opener they've settled onto the new weed edges, working out from the flooded shallows toward open water.

### How it fishes
The season is a migration between the two ends of the reservoir. Spring belongs to the upper arms: the refill floods vegetated banks, pike spawn there, then hold the weed and flat edges while the water is high, with yellow perch and other forage packed into the same cover. Then the drawdown starts in late June and doesn't quit until winter, pulling water off the arms and concentrating everything downstream toward the dam. By ice season the fishery has collapsed onto the dam basin at the west end — the deepest water left — where anglers take pike from the lowest holes, around 15 feet down, often while working perch. Treat any shallow spot as temporary; the reservoir can strand it in weeks. Follow the deep water toward the dam as the season drains the arms.

### Structure
- **Dam basin (deepest water, west end near Wildwood Dam)**: The lowest-lying water holds through the winter drawdown, so it becomes the pike's cold-season address — ice anglers pull them from the deepest holes, around 15 feet, while targeting perch. (Angler-documented; no bathymetric chart exists for the reservoir.)
- **Spring-refill drawdown-zone shallows (upper/eastern reaches of the S-shaped valley)**: Sandy and muddy flooded banks that re-inundate each spring as the reservoir climbs to summer pool — fresh drowned vegetation that lines up with the pike spawn. Specific bays aren't named in the sources; this is the reservoir-cycle pattern, not a mapped spot.

## Yellow Perch
**Parent slug:** yellow-perch
**Tier:** Strong

### Lede
The dam wall is the winter address. When the reservoir sits at its low holding level, perch bunch into the basin right off the wall and hold over 12 to 15 feet — the same deep water that keeps the pike fed. It's a roadside fishery: park near the dam, walk out, and drill a line of holes from about 8 feet out to 15 to find the band the school has settled on.

### How it fishes
Perch are catchable year-round, but the documented fishery here is the winter one, and it turns on the deepest water left after drawdown. As the reservoir falls toward its roughly 3-metre holding level through fall, the perch retreat to the dam-wall basin and stack over 12 to 15 feet. Work minnows just off bottom — the schools hold a foot or two up, and a line of holes from 8 out to 15 feet lets you pin the exact depth they've settled on. That same basin doubles as the pike's pantry, so expect the odd northern from the deepest holes on the same rig. Open water spreads the perch back across the reservoir's flats and weed edges, but no named warm-season structure is documented — winter, on the dam basin, is where the pattern lives.

### Structure
- **Dam-wall basin, 12–15 ft**: The deep water directly off the dam is the documented winter schooling area — a line of ice holes from 8 feet out to 15 found perch concentrated over 12 to 15 feet on minnow rigs, with roadside parking near the dam. (Angler-documented; no bathymetric chart exists for the reservoir.)
